01 — Business Problem

Why does a business need to segment its customers?

Imagine you run an online store with thousands of customers. Some people buy every month and spend a lot. Some bought once a year ago and never came back. Some spend huge amounts in a single order but rarely return.

If you send the same marketing email to all of them — the same discount code, the same message — you are wasting money and missing the point. A loyal customer deserves a "thank you." A customer who hasn't bought in six months needs a "we miss you" offer. A big spender deserves a VIP experience.

This project answers one clear question: "Who are our customers, how are they different from each other, and what should we do specifically for each group — based on their actual behaviour, not guesswork?"

Before this project
One message, everyone gets it
The marketing team sends identical campaigns to all 4,285 customers. No way to know who is loyal, who is drifting, and who is already gone.
After this project
7 groups, 7 strategies
Every customer gets a segment label. The team knows exactly what action to take — from rewarding the best buyers to winning back the lost ones.

03 — Data Cleaning

Removing the noise — about 23% of rows had to go

Real data is always messy. Before running any analysis, the dataset needed careful cleaning. Nearly 1 in 4 rows was removed — not randomly, but for specific, documented reasons. This is one of the most important parts of any data project.

Why does cleaning matter so much? If you build a customer segmentation model on cancelled orders, admin adjustments, and anonymous transactions, every insight you get will be wrong. Bad data in → bad segments out.

// rows remaining after each cleaning step
525461 raw → 406309 final.
Step 01
Remove cancelled invoices
Invoice IDs starting with C are customer returns. They have negative quantities and no real revenue. Dropped 10,209 rows.
Step 02
Remove admin adjustments
Three rows start with A — these are internal bad-debt write-offs with extreme prices like −£53,594. Not real customer transactions.
Step 03
Remove non-product stock codes
Codes like DOT, M, BANK CHARGES, TEST001, AMAZONFEE are operational entries, not products sold to customers. Removed 2,456 rows.
Step 04
Remove missing Customer IDs
107,927 rows have no Customer ID — they are anonymous. Since the whole goal is to segment known customers, these are useless and must be removed.
Step 05
Remove zero-price rows
28 rows had a price of exactly £0.00 — likely free samples or data entry errors. Keeping them would make some customers look like they spent nothing.
Result
406,309 clean rows
77.3% of the original data was kept. What remains is a set of real, paid, identified customer purchases — ready for analysis.
python · data_cleaning.py
cleaned_df = df.copy()

# Step 1: Keep only standard 6-digit numeric invoice IDs
# This removes C-prefix (cancellations) and A-prefix (adjustments)
cleaned_df["Invoice"] = cleaned_df["Invoice"].astype("str")
mask = cleaned_df["Invoice"].str.match(r"^\d{6}$")
cleaned_df = cleaned_df[mask]   # 515,252 rows remain

# Step 2: Keep only real product stock codes
stock_mask = (
    cleaned_df["StockCode"].str.match(r"^\d{5}$")          # e.g. 22824
    | cleaned_df["StockCode"].str.match(r"^\d{5}[a-zA-Z]+$") # e.g. 85123A
    | cleaned_df["StockCode"].str.match(r"^PADS$")            # legitimate product
)
cleaned_df = cleaned_df[stock_mask]   # 512,796 rows remain

# Step 3: Drop rows where Customer ID is missing
cleaned_df.dropna(subset=["Customer ID"], inplace=True)  # 406,437 remain

# Step 4: Remove zero-price rows (free samples / data errors)
cleaned_df = cleaned_df[cleaned_df["Price"] > 0.0]   # 406,309 remain

print(f"Retained: {len(cleaned_df)/len(df):.1%} of raw data")
# > Retained: 77.3% of raw data
04 — Feature Engineering

The RFM model — three numbers that describe every customer

After cleaning, each customer still has many rows of transaction history. Machine learning works on one profile per customer — not hundreds of rows. The RFM model is a classic marketing science technique that collapses all of a customer's history into exactly three numbers.

R
Recency
How many days have passed since their last purchase? A low number means they bought recently and are engaged. A high number means they may have drifted away.
F
Frequency
How many separate orders have they placed? A customer who returns many times is loyal and habitual. A one-time buyer is casual or at risk of leaving.
M
Monetary Value
How much have they spent in total (£)? High spenders deserve premium treatment. Low spenders may need upselling to increase their basket size.
python · feature_engineering.py
# Calculate revenue per line item
cleaned_df["SalesLineTotal"] = cleaned_df["Quantity"] * cleaned_df["Price"]

# Collapse from transaction-level to customer-level (one row per customer)
aggregated_df = cleaned_df.groupby(by="Customer ID", as_index=False).agg(
    MonetaryValue = ("SalesLineTotal", "sum"),     # total £ spent
    Frequency     = ("Invoice",       "nunique"),  # number of unique orders
    LastInvoiceDate = ("InvoiceDate", "max")      # date of most recent order
)

# Calculate recency: days between last purchase and dataset end date (2010-12-09)
max_date = aggregated_df["LastInvoiceDate"].max()
aggregated_df["Recency"] = (max_date - aggregated_df["LastInvoiceDate"]).dt.days

05 — Outlier Detection

Separating the extreme customers before clustering

Before running KMeans, a key decision was made: remove extreme outliers first. Here is why this matters — KMeans tries to find balanced, round cluster centres. If you include a customer who spent £349,000 alongside customers who spent £300, the algorithm will distort every cluster to account for that one extreme case. Everyone else gets misclassified.

The IQR method was used — the same mathematical method that powers box plots. Any customer more than 1.5× the interquartile range above the third quartile in Monetary Value or Frequency was flagged as an outlier.

Outlier threshold = Q3 + (1.5 × IQR)  where  IQR = Q3 − Q1 Applied separately to Monetary Value and Frequency. Outliers are not deleted — they get their own special segments.
python · outlier_detection.py
# --- Monetary outliers ---
M_Q1 = aggregated_df["MonetaryValue"].quantile(0.25)
M_Q3 = aggregated_df["MonetaryValue"].quantile(0.75)
M_IQR = M_Q3 - M_Q1

monetary_outliers = aggregated_df[
    aggregated_df["MonetaryValue"] > (M_Q3 + 1.5 * M_IQR)
]  # 423 customers — avg spend: £12,188

# --- Frequency outliers ---
F_Q1 = aggregated_df["Frequency"].quantile(0.25)
F_Q3 = aggregated_df["Frequency"].quantile(0.75)
F_IQR = F_Q3 - F_Q1

frequency_outliers = aggregated_df[
    aggregated_df["Frequency"] > (F_Q3 + 1.5 * F_IQR)
]  # 279 customers — avg frequency: 23 orders

# Keep non-outlier customers for KMeans
non_outliers = aggregated_df[
    ~aggregated_df.index.isin(monetary_outliers.index)
    & ~aggregated_df.index.isin(frequency_outliers.index)
]  # 3,809 customers — this is what KMeans will cluster

06 — KMeans Clustering

Finding the natural customer groups using machine learning

With 3,809 customers each described by three RFM numbers, KMeans clustering was applied. The algorithm works by finding a set number of "centre points" in the data and assigning each customer to the nearest one. Customers close to the same centre end up in the same group.

Step 1 — Why we must scale the data first

Monetary values go up to £3,788. Frequency goes up to 11. Recency goes up to 373. KMeans uses distance to group points — so the big monetary numbers would completely dominate and frequency would be almost ignored. Standard scaling fixes this by converting each feature to have mean = 0 and standard deviation = 1, so all three contribute equally.

z = (x − μ) / σ z = scaled value · x = original value · μ = mean of that feature · σ = standard deviation
python · clustering.py
# Scale the 3 features so no single one dominates KMeans
scaler = StandardScaler()
scaled_data = scaler.fit_transform(
    non_outliers[["MonetaryValue", "Frequency", "Recency"]]
)
scaled_df = pd.DataFrame(scaled_data, index=non_outliers.index,
                           columns=["MonetaryValue", "Frequency", "Recency"])

# --- Find the best number of clusters (k=2 to k=12) ---
inertia_scores, silhouette_scores = [], []
k_values = range(2, 13)

for k in k_values:
    km = KMeans(n_clusters=k, random_state=42, max_iter=1000)
    labels = km.fit_predict(scaled_df)
    inertia_scores.append(km.inertia_)
    silhouette_scores.append(silhouette_score(scaled_df, labels))

# Both metrics agree: k=4 is the right choice
# Inertia shows a clear "elbow" at k=4
# Silhouette score peaks at k=4 (highest = most distinct groups)

# --- Fit the final model with k=4 ---
kmeans = KMeans(n_clusters=4, random_state=42, max_iter=1000)
non_outliers["Cluster"] = kmeans.fit_predict(scaled_df)

Step 2 — Choosing the right number of clusters

The algorithm needs to be told how many groups to create. Two methods were used to figure this out — and both pointed to the same answer: k = 4.

// inertia (elbow method) — bend occurs at k=4
Elbow visible at k=4.
// silhouette score — peaks at k=4
Peak silhouette score at k=4.

What is the silhouette score? It measures how well each customer fits into their assigned cluster compared to all other clusters. A score closer to 1 means the groups are very distinct and well-separated. Scoring k=2 through k=12 and picking the peak is the data-driven way to choose — not guessing.

s(i) = [ b(i) − a(i) ] / max( a(i), b(i) ) a(i) = avg distance to own cluster · b(i) = avg distance to nearest other cluster · Range: −1 to +1

07 — Customer Segments

The 7 customer segments and what to do with each

The four KMeans clusters (for regular customers) plus three outlier groups give us seven named segments. Each one has a clear description and a specific, actionable business strategy. The names were chosen so that any marketer or manager — not just a data scientist — can understand and act on them immediately.

08 — Insights & Recommendations

What the analysis actually tells us

Here are the most important things learned from this project — explained in plain terms, not just data science jargon.

📉
1 in 4 rows in the raw data was not a real sale
23% of the dataset — about 119,000 rows — had to be removed before any analysis could begin. These were cancellations, admin entries, postage charges, and anonymous transactions. This is completely normal in real business data. The lesson: cleaning is not a quick step you rush through. If you had skipped it and built segments on raw data, every result would have been misleading.
💰
A tiny number of customers likely generate most of the revenue
The DELIGHT segment is just 111 customers — about 2.6% of the total base. But based on their average spend and frequency, they likely account for around 38% of total revenue. This is the classic 80/20 rule in action. If a business loses 10 DELIGHT customers, it could cost more revenue than losing 500 RE-ENGAGE customers. This tells you where to focus your energy first.
⏱️
Recency is the earliest warning that a customer is leaving
The RE-ENGAGE cluster is defined mainly by high recency — a long time since their last purchase. If a business tracks this number in real time (not just once a year in a project like this), they can catch customers the moment they start drifting — and send a targeted campaign to bring them back before they are fully gone.
🔀
Separating outliers before clustering was the right call
If the DELIGHT and PAMPER customers had been included when running KMeans, the algorithm would have stretched all four clusters toward them — making the regular customer segments blurry and less useful. By identifying and separating outliers first using IQR, the four main clusters became much cleaner. Good methodology produces results you can actually trust and act on.
✅
Two validation methods confirming k=4 removes guesswork
Many clustering projects simply pick a number of clusters and hope for the best. Here, both the elbow method (inertia) and the silhouette score independently agreed that k=4 produces the most well-separated, meaningful groups. When two different methods give the same answer, the result is trustworthy — not lucky.
🌍
92% of transactions came from the UK — international is an untapped opportunity
The dataset skews almost entirely UK-based, with 39 other countries making up a tiny share. A follow-up project could segment international customers separately to understand which products travel well, which countries have growth potential, and whether buying behaviour differs from UK customers.
📊
The UPSELL segment shows that frequency and spend don't always move together
The UPSELL customers are buying more frequently than almost anyone — but spending very little per visit. This is actually a great sign: they trust the brand and keep coming back. The right move is not to push them to visit more often (they already do), but to give them a reason to buy more in each visit through bundles, upgrade prompts, or complementary products.
